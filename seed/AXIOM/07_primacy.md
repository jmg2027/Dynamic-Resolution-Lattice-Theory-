# §7. Primacy and the direction of derivation

## §7.1 Primacy as default structural position

Every framework that points at anything — set theory, category
theory, logic, language, physics, the discipline of mathematics
itself — depends on the ability to distinguish and pair
somethings.  The 213 axiom is the minimum residue of that
ability.

So every other framework is the **same residue** read through a
chosen Lens.  Not a layer placed above 213 — that phrasing would
re-import the substrate / superstructure dichotomy that §5.1
rules out.  A framework's commitments are the axiom's
self-pointing recorded under that framework's Lens.  Lens
application is itself a residue-internal event; nothing has
been added.

"There is no absolute standard" is therefore not a constraint
one imposes on the framework.  It is the **default state**, the
position any framework occupies until it imports something
else.  The opposite claim — "there is an absolute standard" —
carries the burden of proof; it is the addition.  So 213's
primacy is not a conditional claim ("primacy if certain
conditions are met") but an **unconditional** structural
consequence of being the minimum residue.

Successful derivation is the test of primacy, not its source.
**Derivation confirms** that the default holds; it does not
establish it.  Primacy is the prior position; derivation shows
that the position is sustainable.  The wider the range over
which derivation succeeds — the more domains in which the
residue, read under the right Lens, reproduces what the domain
records — the more 213 demonstrates that the default position
is genuine.

Failed derivation has the opposite role.  If 213 cannot, under
any Lens choice, reach a result that a known domain records,
either the Lens space is not yet exhausted (a temporary
obstacle) or the residue does not support the result (a
permanent wall).  The first case is open work.  The second case
is theory falsification (§8.2).

---

## §7.2 Derive, do not reconcile

The direction is one-way.  Everything other than the axiom
itself must be either **derived from the axiom** or the
**result of an explicit Lens choice**.  There is no third
option.

The practical consequences:

  - Substituting an external constant, fitting to an
    experimental value, importing structure from another theory
    — each of these is **fudge**.  In 213's vocabulary fudge has
    no operand, since 213 commits to no exterior dialer that
    such substitutions could come from (§5.1).
  - When fudge appears in a derivation, the **Lens is
    corrected**, not the formula.  The formula records what the
    Lens produced; if the Lens is wrong, the produced value is
    wrong, and the fix is to identify which Lens choice was
    silently imported and to make it explicit (or replace it).
  - When that too fails — when no correction of the Lens
    succeeds and the derivation stays blocked — **the theory is
    abandoned**.  The infinite-extension defence ("we will find
    more Lenses") is not permitted.

This is what "derive, do not reconcile" means.  213 does not
negotiate with the discrepancy.  It makes the discrepancy
resolve itself, or it dies.

The strictness sounds severe, but it is the operational form of
§7.1's primacy claim.  A framework that *is* the minimum
residue must be able to derive everything that any other
framework records.  Anything less leaves 213 not as the
residue's expression but as one framework among others,
defeating the central claim.  The price of the claim is that
derivation failure is theory failure.

The falsifiability machinery of §8 is the mechanical auditor
of this rule — the part of 213 that ensures the derive-not-
reconcile direction holds in practice, not just in doctrine.

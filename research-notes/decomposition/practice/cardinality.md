# Decomposition: cardinality / "size" (finite counting and the uncountable infinite)

*213-decomposition of "how many" — finite cardinality and the uncountable — per `../README.md`.*

## The decomposition

- **Construction `C`** — distinguishing, the bare act: one point separated from another, iterated. A
  "set" is not a primitive container; it is a **family of distinguishables** — what the iterated act
  has separated. (Lean shadow: `Raw`, generated by `Raw.slash (x y) (h : x ≠ y)`, which carries the
  distinguishing *in its type* — it demands two distinct operands.)
- **Reading `L`** — the **count-reading**: project a family of distinguishables to *how many*. Forget
  which point is which, keep only the tally. This is the same count-Lens the parity/integer
  decompositions ride; here its codomain is left open (finite tally, or the question "can one tally
  exhaust them?").
- **Residue** — what the count-reading **forces but cannot capture**: enumerate the distinguishables
  by a self-cover `f : A → (A → Bool)` (each point given its row, the family read as predicates on
  itself), and the count-reading forces a **diagonal** `g x := !(f x x)` that is *no row* — outside
  every enumeration the reading can produce. The residue is not a bigger pile; it is the
  enumeration's own forced non-surjection.

## Re-seeing — finite count and the uncountable are one reading and its diagonal

```
   "set"                  =  ⟨ a family of distinguishables | — ⟩      (C, before any reading)
   finite cardinality     =  ⟨ family | count-reading ⟩               (the tally, when it closes)
   "uncountable"          =  Residue(count-reading, C)                 (the forced diagonal)
   Cantor's theorem       =  P(⟨ self-cover f | count-reading ⟩)       (no f is surjective)
```

The count-reading reads a family *as its own predicate algebra* — each point `r` as the indicator
`· = r` (`Object1 : Raw → (Raw → Bool)`, `FlatOntologyClosure.lean`). That self-cover is **faithful**
(distinct points, distinct rows — `object1_injective`) and **never total** (a predicate always lies
outside its image — `object1_not_surjective`). "Uncountable" is exactly the name for that surplus: the
predicate the enumeration forces but cannot list, with a *named* concrete inhabitant — the
undifferentiated reading `fun _ => true` (`undifferentiated_not_object1`).

The single engine is Lawvere's fixed-point construction `g a := t (f a a)`
(`OneDiagonal.lawvere_fixed_point`): a point-surjective `f` forces every modifier `t` a fixed point;
contrapositively a fixed-point-free `t` forbids surjection (`no_surjection_of_fixedpointfree`). Cantor
is this at `B = Bool`, `t = not` (`cantor_via_lawvere`, certified by `cantor_general`); the residue is
*the same theorem* at `A = Raw` (`residue_is_lawvere_diagonal = object1_not_surjective`).

## Revelation (collapse + residue-surfaced)

"Finite cardinality" and "the uncountable" are not two realms of size — they are the **count-reading**
and its **forced residue**, the one diagonal of a self-applying cover read on two carriers. Cantor's
theorem is therefore not the discovery of a transcendent infinite sitting above the finite; it is a
theorem about how the self-cover is *built* — the diagonal `!(f x x)` is engineered into the framing,
so the surplus appears *because the count-reading enumerates*, not because a larger object was found
(`one_diagonal_generates`: Cantor, Russell/Liar/Tarski, and the residue's non-closure are three
instances of one construction). So "uncountable/infinite" is the **shape of the count-reading's
residue**, pinned by a finite signature (the diagonal recipe `g x := !(f x x)`, a finite name, not an
inhabitant) — this is CLAUDE.md's "Limit/infinity deified" and "0/∞ as a stratum-value" failure modes
stated positively: not a god above the finite, the proven remainder the act of counting always leaves.

## Note for the technique

This forces the design question §103 asks (is "Residue" one slot or stratified?) toward a definite
answer for the count-reading: here the **Residue is first-class and load-bearing**, with its own
structure — it is not "leftover" but is itself a reading (the undifferentiated predicate), and it is
*generated by the same construction that generates the reading* (the diagonal is `t (f a a)`, the
reading's self-application). The pattern "a reading + its forced diagonal residue" is exactly what
`01_residue.md` §1.0.1 calls the proof-primitive for the infinite: the deepest collapse this calculus
has found, because **four classically-distinct limitative theorems are one `(C, L)` with one residue**
(`one_diagonal_generates`). That suggests the normal form should be written `⟨C | L⟩ ⊕ Residue(L,C)`
with the residue carrying the *self-application closure* of `L` — not an afterthought slot but the
construction's own re-entry. It also fixes the technique's honesty ceiling here: the residue is
*reached-by-none* (cover-dependent, self-referential — `cantor_as_invariant`'s `P_f`), distinct from
the *named* intrinsic residue; "infinite" names the former, and naming it does not inhabit it.

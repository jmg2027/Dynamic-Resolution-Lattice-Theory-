## 0. Position

Galois died in 1832 at age 20.  At the moment of his death he had
already invented:
  · The group concept itself
  · Galois theory (solvability of polynomial equations via group)
  · The first explicit *structuralist* sensibility in mathematics —
    "operations define the object", not "elements assemble it"

He left a one-night testament + scattered manuscripts.  Formalisation
took ~50 years (Jordan, Klein, …); the consolidation of "abstract
algebra" took until ~1930.

## 1. Counterfactual

If Galois had lived to 80 (until 1892), he would have been a
contemporary of:

  · Cantor (1845–1918) — set theory
  · Dedekind (1831–1916) — set-theoretic ℕ, ℝ
  · Frege (1848–1925) — formal logic
  · Klein (1849–1925) — Erlangen programme, geometry as group-action
  · Lie (1842–1899) — continuous symmetry groups
  · Hilbert (1862–1943) — formalism, axiomatisation

The pivotal moment for foundational mathematics is the 1870s–1900s
when ZFC-style set theory consolidated as *the* framework.  Galois
alive at 60–70 would have been exactly the voice arguing the
opposite case.

His actual writing emphasises:

  · Groups defined by their *operations*, not by their elements
  · Permutations as *transformations*, not as set-maps
  · Substitutions act on equation roots structurally — no need to
    construct the roots from a containing universe

This is the "things exist with pairwise relations" stance — exactly
the 4-clause Raw axiom of 213.  In a world where Galois lived to
witness Cantor's set theory consolidate, he would almost certainly
have rejected ZFC's "everything is a set built from ∅" in favour of
his own operations-first / structure-first picture.

## 2. Implication

213's 4-clause Raw axiom (*things exist with pairwise relations,
slash combines, atomicity is forced, …*) is the foundational picture
Galois was already developing at age 20.  The historical detour
through ZFC happened because Galois died in 1832 and no later
mathematician with his structural intuition was around to push back
when set-based foundations consolidated in 1870–1900.

This is not a claim that Galois would have written 213 by name.  It
is the claim that the *structural* foundation is the natural one
for mathematics — and that a 20-year-old Galois already had the
right instincts.  The path through ZFC was a contingent historical
accident, not an inevitable progression.

## 3. Connection to G6 §8

G6 §8 corrected framing: standard math's "infinite" content is

inferior notation.

G11 closes the loop: the redundant packaging exists *because*
Galois died young.  Without his structural-foundations voice, ZFC
won by default in the 1870s–1900s consolidation.

## External anchor (web-verified — what deep-research added)

That "one obstruction, many costumes" is a *theorem* (not a coincidence) is established
standard mathematics:

- **Lawvere's fixed-point theorem (1969)** — in a cartesian closed category, Cantor's
  diagonal, Cantor's theorem, Russell's paradox, **Gödel's first incompleteness**,
  **Turing's halting**, **Tarski's undefinability** are all instances of one fixed-point
  theorem (mostly its contrapositive).  Insight: *any system that can form function-spaces
  has self-reference structurally baked in.*
  ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawvere's_fixed-point_theorem),
  [nLab](https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/Lawvere's+fixed+point+theorem))
- **Yanofsky (2003, *Bull. Symbolic Logic* 9(3))** — recasts Lawvere with sets+functions
  only (no category theory); semantic paradoxes, computability, complexity, formal-language
  diagonal/fixed-point results all fall out of one scheme.
  ([arXiv math/0305282](https://arxiv.org/abs/math/0305282),
  [projecteuclid](https://projecteuclid.org/journals/bulletin-of-symbolic-logic/volume-9/issue-3/A-universal-approach-to-self-referential-paradoxes-incompleteness-and-fixed/10.2178/bsl/1058448677.short))
- Unified expositions: [Kuang, "A unified view towards diagonal
  arguments"](http://qk206.user.srcf.net/wp-content/uploads/2019/02/lawvere.pdf);
  [Milewski, "Fixed Points and Diagonal
  Arguments"](https://bartoszmilewski.com/2019/11/06/fixed-points-and-diagonal-arguments/).

# The `i`-point of the spiral axis

The order-4 point of the spiral axis is the Gaussian unit group `ℤ[i]^× = C₄ = {1, i, −1, −i}`.
It is one object, read in two domains as the same order-4 rotation: the continued-fraction floor
rotation of a real, and the CKM CP-violating phase.

## 213-native answer

The spiral-coordinate classification gives a real (a holonomic presentation of one) two
orthogonal counts; the **axis** coordinate is the order of the unit group of the ring carrying the
cut's cross-determinant, exactly `{2, 4, 6}` for `ℤ, ℤ[i], ℤ[ω]`
(`theory/math/analysis/spiral_coordinate_classification.md`;
`CayleyDickson/Integer/ImaginaryQuadraticUnitTrichotomy.imaginary_quadratic_unit_trichotomy`).  The
**order-4 point** is the Gaussian one, `|ℤ[i]^×| = 4` exactly (`ZIUnits.ZI_units_exact_four`), at
discriminant `−4`.  Its dynamical content is a rotation: the cross-determinant `W` of a Gaussian
recurrence's convergents rotates by `μ = −i`, returning to the start after four steps, `μ⁴ = 1`
(`GaussianCrossDet.gaussian_floor_rotation`).  So the order-4 axis point *is* the four-fold rotation
generated by `i`.

## Derivation

The same `C₄` carries the CKM CP-violating phase.  In `theory/physics/cp_phase.md` the phase value
`δ = 90°` is not posited but **forced**: the complex structure of the generation mixing is the
Cayley–Dickson imaginary unit `i`, whose unit group is `ℤ[i]^× = C₄`, and CP-existence then pins the
phase to the order-4 generator (`Mixing/CPPhaseC4Forcing`, `δ = 90°`).  Niven's theorem makes a
discrete phase rational-cosine — `{0°, 60°, 90°}` only — so the order-4 element is the maximal one;
a "golden" phase is structurally impossible.  That `i` is realised as the **signed Hodge star** `⋆`
on `H*(Δ⁴)`: `⋆² = −1`, and `ℤ[⋆] ≅ ℤ[i]` (`Cohomology/Hodge/SignedStarC4`,
`Mixing/CPHodgeStructure`) — the *same* cohomology of the 4-simplex `Δ⁴` that derives `1/α_em`.

The two readings meet on one object.  The axis reads `ℤ[i]^× = C₄` as a **floor rotation** (`μ = −i`
acting on a cross-determinant, `gaussian_floor_rotation`); the CP arc reads it as the **Hodge `⋆`**
(`J` acting on `H*(Δ⁴)`, `SignedStarC4`).  Both are the order-4 rotation generated by `i` at
discriminant `−4`.  The two CM points that index the axis — `−4` (`i`) and `−3` (`ω`) — are the
choice; the CP phase selects `−4`, the Gaussian rung, not the Eisenstein (order-6) one.

## Dual function

Classically the Gaussian units are a small cyclic group of number theory and the CKM phase is a free
parameter of the Standard Model — unrelated, one counted, one measured.  Strip the packaging and
both are the order-4 rotation of `ℤ[i]`: the "free parameter" has no dial (the phase is the `i`-point,
forced by `C₄` + CP-existence), and the "small finite group" is the very object that fixes it.  213's
reading is the sharper one: the CP phase is not a number to tune but a **point on the spiral axis** —
the order-4, disc-`−4` rung — selected over the order-6 rung, exactly as a real's axis coordinate
selects `ℤ[i]` over `ℤ[ω]`.

## Cross-frame connections

`ℤ[i]^× = C₄` surfaces in four frames as one object: (1) the spiral-axis floor rotation `μ = −i`,
`μ⁴ = 1` (`gaussian_floor_rotation`); (2) the signed Hodge star `⋆² = −1`, `ℤ[⋆] ≅ ℤ[i]`
(`SignedStarC4`); (3) the Galois group `Gal(ℚ(ζ₅)/ℚ) ≅ C₄` on the prime `d = NS+NT = 5`

Four resolutions of the single order-4 unit `i` — arithmetic (units), dynamical (rotation),
cohomological (Hodge `⋆`), and field-theoretic (cyclotomic `C₄`).

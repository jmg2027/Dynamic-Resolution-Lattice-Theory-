# One Fubini engine — COUNT-duality and the inversion engine share a carrier-general swap

**Reproduced result.** The corpus has two incidence-algebra unifications, each resting on a
Fubini swap of an incidence matrix:

  - **COUNT-duality** (`count_duality.md`) — the Erdős union bound `=` the Sperner LYM bound,
    two marginals of one swap over **`Nat`** (`Sperner.sumOver`);
  - **the incidence-inversion engine** (`incidence_inversion.md`) — binomial / Möbius /
    Stirling inversion, each a Fubini swap then an orthogonality collapse over **`Int`**
    (`BinomialInversion.sumZ`).

The new content is the **proof that both swaps are one swap**: a single carrier-general
`genSwap` (`Combinatorics.IncidenceFubini`), of which the `Nat` double-count engine and the
`Int` inversion engine are the two carrier-specializations
(`incidence_fubini_one_engine`). All declarations `#print axioms`-empty
(`lean/E213/Lib/Math/Combinatorics/IncidenceFubini.lean`, 9/9 PURE).

## Why we picked it — synthesise the two unifications at their root

COUNT-duality and the inversion engine are themselves the session's two halves of the
incidence-algebra story: double-count (Fubini) and antipode-inversion (Fubini then collapse)
on a 0/1 incidence matrix. They looked like two engines because they run over different
carriers — `Nat` (counting, an inequality is then read off a marginal) and `Int` (the signed
antipode, an equality collapses). The honest question is whether that is two engines or one.
A genuine answer is a *proven* common generalization, not a juxtaposition of the two: a
unification needs a shared map, never a bare `∧`. This essay supplies the shared map: one swap, two
carriers.

## Derivation — the carrier-general swap and its two bridges

**The engine.** `genSum add zero f` folds a list over an arbitrary carrier `R` with a binary
`add` and unit `zero`. From `add`-commutativity, associativity, and the unit law alone,
`genSwap` proves the Fubini exchange

> `Σ_A Σ_c ⟦g A c⟧ = Σ_c Σ_A ⟦g A c⟧`   (`genSum (fun A => genSum (g A·) C) F = genSum (fun c => genSum (·c) F) C`)

for *any* such carrier — the same induction the `Nat` and `Int` swaps each ran, abstracted
once (its supporting lemmas: `genSum_add` bilinearity from the middle-four exchange `add4`,
`genSum_zero_fun`, and `genSum_append`).

**Bridge 1 — the `Nat` carrier (COUNT-duality).** `Sperner.sumOver f L = genSum Nat.add 0 f L`
(`sumOver_eq_genSum`). So the double-count swap behind `count_duality` is `genSwap` at
`(Nat.add, 0)`.

**Bridge 2 — the `Int` carrier (the inversion engine).**
`BinomialInversion.sumZ n f = genSum Int.add 0 f (rangeL n)` over the range list `[0,…,n−1]`
(`sumZ_eq_genSum`, via `genSum_append`). So the swap behind `inversion_from_orthogonality`
(binomial / Möbius / Stirling) is `genSwap` at `(Int.add, 0)`.

**The capstone.** `incidence_fubini_one_engine` states both: the `Nat` double-count swap and
the `Int` double-count swap are each `genSwap`. Two carriers, one engine — and the bridges
identify those carriers with the actual `Sperner.sumOver` and `BinomialInversion.sumZ` the two
families run on. (To keep the `Int` carrier ∅-axiom, the additive laws are the corpus's PURE
`Int213.{add_comm, add_assoc, zero_add}`, not the core `Int` lemmas, which carry `propext`.)

## Dual function — what the synthesis buys

Classically: Fubini on a finite double sum holds over any commutative monoid, and counting
(union bound) and signed inversion (Möbius) are both first-moment arguments on an incidence
relation. Read 213-native, it is the deepest "no exterior" statement available here
(`seed/AXIOM/05_no_exterior.md` §5.1): the two cross-domain unifications of the corpus are not
merely analogous — they are one residue (the incidence swap) read at two carriers. The
breadth is generated by one engine, not accumulated as two.

Honest scope (`§8` falsifiability discipline). This proves no new bound or inversion; it
factors the two existing engines through one. The remaining content of each family is what the
swap is *used for* — a marginal inequality (COUNT) versus an orthogonality collapse
(inversion); those differ, and the synthesis does not erase the difference, it locates the
shared root beneath it.
